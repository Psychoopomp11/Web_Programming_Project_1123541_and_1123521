8. Bali Starling

Leucopsar rothschildi

The Bali Starling, or Jalak Bali, is one of the rarest and most beautiful birds in the world. Native to Bali, Indonesia, this bird is instantly recognizable for its striking white feathers, blue skin around its eyes, and black wing tips. Males display their bright plumage to attract females, and their enchanting songs can often be heard echoing through their natural habitats, which are the tropical forests of Bali.

At around 25 centimeters (9.8 inches) in length, the Bali Starling is a relatively small bird, and it feeds primarily on fruits, seeds, and insects. Unfortunately, the Bali Starling is critically endangered, with fewer than 100 individuals remaining in the wild. Habitat destruction, the illegal pet trade, and human encroachment have all contributed to its decline. Conservation programs, including captive breeding and reintroduction efforts, are in place to help restore the population.

To protect the Bali Starling, the Bali Bird Park and other conservation organizations are working to create safe environments and enforce stricter regulations against poaching.

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s):Bali Starling
  • Scientific Name: Leucopsar rothschildi
  • Animal Type:: Bird
  • Size: 25 centimeters (9.8 inches) in length
  • Weight: 100 grams (3.5 ounces)
  • Lifespan: 20 to 30 years in captivity

9. Kalimantan Red Cat

Pardofelis badia

The Kalimantan Red Cat, or Kucing Merah Kalimantan, is a mysterious and little-known species of wild cat native to the rainforests of Borneo, particularly in the Kalimantan region of Indonesia. This small, elusive cat is known for its reddish-brown fur and large, expressive eyes. It is a solitary animal, typically seen only during the night when it ventures out to hunt for small prey such as rodents, birds, and insects.

With a body length of around 50 centimeters (19.7 inches), the Kalimantan Red Cat is an agile and stealthy predator. Due to its secretive nature and the dense forests it inhabits, little is known about its behavior or lifestyle. However, it is believed to be a solitary and territorial animal. It faces threats from habitat loss and fragmentation due to deforestation and illegal logging.

Efforts to protect the Kalimantan Red Cat are challenging due to the lack of research and awareness, but local conservation initiatives aim to preserve its rainforest habitat and reduce human-wildlife conflict.

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s):Kalimantan Red Cat
  • Scientific Name: Pardofelis badia
  • Animal Type:: Mammal
  • Size: 50 centimeters (19.7 inches) in body length
  • Weight: 2 to 3 kilograms (4.4 to 6.6 pounds)
  • Lifespan: 10 to 12 years in the wild

10. Sumatran Elephant

Elephas maximus sumatranus

The Sumatran Elephant, or Gajah Sumatra, is a subspecies of the Asian elephant found only on the Indonesian island of Sumatra. These elephants are smaller than their mainland relatives, with males typically weighing around 4,000 to 5,000 kilograms (8,800 to 11,000 pounds) and standing about 2.5 meters (8.2 feet) tall at the shoulder. The Sumatran Elephant is known for its rounder, more compact shape and its gentle demeanor.

These elephants are herbivores, feeding on a wide variety of plants, including grasses, leaves, fruits, and bark. They are highly social animals, living in matriarchal herds led by the oldest female. Elephants communicate using vocalizations, body language, and vibrations that travel through the ground. The Sumatran Elephant is critically endangered, with only an estimated 1,000 individuals remaining in the wild.

Threats to the Sumatran Elephant include habitat destruction due to deforestation, human-wildlife conflict, and poaching. Conservation efforts focus on habitat preservation, anti-poaching measures, and working with local communities to mitigate conflicts.

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s):Sumatran Elephant
  • Scientific Name: Elephas maximus sumatranus
  • Animal Type:: Mammal
  • Size: 2.5 meters (8.2 feet) tall at the shoulder
  • Weight: 4,000 to 5,000 kilograms (8,800 to 11,000 pounds)
  • Lifespan: 60 years in the wild
