4. White-bellied Heron

Ardea insignis

White-bellied Heron is a rare and majestic bird, often called the “King of Wetlands” because of its large size and graceful appearance. This bird is one of the largest herons in the world, standing tall at about 127 centimeters (4.2 feet)! It has a stunning white belly and dark grayish-black feathers on its back, wings, and neck, making it easy to recognize.

These herons are shy and prefer to live in quiet, undisturbed freshwater rivers, lakes, and marshes, mainly in the foothills of the Himalayas. They use their long, sharp beaks to hunt for fish, frogs, and small crabs, which they catch by standing still like statues and waiting for the perfect moment to strike.

Sadly, the White-bellied Heron is critically endangered due to habitat loss, pollution, and human activity. There are only a few of them left in the wild, making every sighting of this bird a very special moment.

Fun Fact: White-bellied Herons are so quiet that they are often called "ghost birds" because they are rarely seen or heard in the wild!

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s): White-bellied Heron
  • Scientific Name: Ardea insignis
  • Animal Type: Bird
  • Size: Up to 127 cm (4.2 ft)
  • Weight: About 4-5 kg (8.8-11 lbs)
  • Lifespan: 20-30 years in the wild
  • Conservation Status: Critically Endangered

5. Great Indian Bustard

Ardeotis nigriceps

Great Indian Bustard is a giant bird often called the "Guardian of the Grasslands" because of its vital role in its ecosystem. This magnificent bird stands tall at about 1 meter (3.3 feet) and weighs up to 15 kilograms (33 pounds), making it one of the heaviest flying birds in the world. It has a striking appearance with a black crown on its head, a white neck, and a sandy-brown body that blends perfectly with dry grasslands.

Great Indian Bustards live in semi-arid regions, preferring open landscapes like grasslands and scrublands. They feed on a mix of seeds, berries, insects, and small reptiles, making them important for controlling pests in their habitat. Unfortunately, this bird is critically endangered due to habitat destruction, hunting, and collisions with power lines. Conservationists are working hard to protect its remaining population.

Fun Fact: When it's time to attract a mate, male Great Indian Bustards perform an elaborate dance by puffing up their necks and strutting around to impress the females. It's like a bird fashion show!

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s): Great Indian Bustard
  • Scientific Name: Ardeotis nigriceps
  • Animal Type: Bird
  • Size: Up to 1 meter (3.3 ft)
  • Weight: Up to 15 kg (33 lbs)
  • Lifespan: 10-15 years in the wild
  • Conservation Status: Critically Endangered

6.Baer's Pochard

Aythya baeri

Baer's Pochard is a medium-sized diving duck that loves swimming in freshwater lakes and ponds. This striking duck has a glossy greenish-black head, a white belly, and a chestnut-brown back, making it stand out on the water. Males are more colorful, while females have a softer brown plumage. These ducks are expert divers and use their strong legs to swim underwater in search of food like aquatic plants, small fish, and insects. They are migratory birds, traveling long distances between breeding and wintering grounds in East and Southeast Asia.

Sadly, Baer's Pochard is critically endangered, with its population declining rapidly due to habitat destruction, hunting, and pollution. Conservation programs are focusing on protecting their wetlands and raising awareness.

Fun Fact: Baer's Pochard can stay underwater for up to 30 seconds while searching for food—a true underwater explorer!

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s): Baer's Pochard
  • Scientific Name: Aythya baeri
  • Animal Type: Bird
  • Size: 41-45 cm (16-18 in.)
  • Weight: 500-900 grams (1.1-2 lbs)
  • Lifespan: 5-10 years in the wild
  • Conservation Status: Critically Endangered

7. Spoon-billed Sandpiper

Calidris pygmaea

Spoon-billed Sandpiper is a tiny shorebird with a unique, spoon-shaped beak that sets it apart from other birds. This special beak helps it scoop up tiny crustaceans and insects in the mudflats where it lives. Spoon-billed Sandpipers are only about 14-16 cm (5.5-6.3 inches) long but are big travelers, migrating thousands of kilometers from their breeding grounds in Siberia to their wintering areas in Southeast Asia.

These adorable birds are critically endangered due to habitat loss along their migration routes and illegal hunting. Protecting their wetlands is crucial for their survival.

Fun Fact: Spoon-billed Sandpipers use their spoon-shaped beaks like tiny shovels, making feeding both efficient and adorable!

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s): Spoon-billed Sandpiper
  • Scientific Name: Calidris pygmaea
  • Animal Type: Bird
  • Size: 14-16 cm (5.5-6.3 in.)
  • Weight: 20-25 grams (0.04-0.06 lbs)
  • Lifespan: 5-8 years in the wild
  • Conservation Status: Critically Endangered
