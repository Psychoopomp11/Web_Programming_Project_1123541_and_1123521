8. Oxus Cobra

Naja oxiana

Oxus Cobra is a venomous snake native to Central Asia, known for its distinctive hood and potent venom. These cobras are usually brown or gray with lighter bands, allowing them to blend into their arid and rocky environments. They are carnivorous, feeding on rodents, birds, and other small animals.

Fun Fact: Oxus Cobras can "stand" by lifting the front part of their body off the ground—a truly intimidating display!

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s): Oxus Cobra
  • Scientific Name: Naja oxiana
  • Animal Type: Reptile
  • Size: 1-1.5 meters (3.3-4.9 ft)
  • Weight: 1-3 kg (2.2-6.6 lbs)
  • Lifespan: 10-12 years in the wild
  • Conservation Status: Vulnerable

9.Latifi’s Viper

Montivipera latifii

Latifi’s Viper is a rare and venomous snake endemic to the Elburz Mountains of Iran. This small viper has a beautiful pattern of zigzag bands on its back, which helps it blend into rocky and mountainous terrain. It is a nocturnal predator, feeding on lizards, rodents, and insects.

Conservation Status: Latifi’s Viper is critically endangered due to its limited range, habitat destruction, and collection for the illegal pet trade. Conservationists are working to protect its unique habitat.

Fun Fact: Latifi’s Viper has heat-sensing pits near its mouth, allowing it to detect warm-blooded prey even in complete darkness!

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s): Latifi’s Viper
  • Scientific Name: Montivipera latifii
  • Animal Type: Reptile
  • Size: 60-80 cm (2-2.6 ft)
  • Weight: 0.5-1 kg (1.1-2.2 lbs)
  • Lifespan: 10-15 years in the wild
  • Conservation Status: Critically Endangered

10. Dugong

Dugong dugon

Dugong is a gentle marine mammal often called the "sea cow" because of its diet of seagrass. These large, slow-moving animals are closely related to manatees and are vital for maintaining healthy seagrass ecosystems. Dugongs have a streamlined body and paddle-like flippers, making them graceful swimmers.

Conservation Status: Dugongs are found in warm coastal waters from East Africa to Australia. They are listed as vulnerable due to habitat loss, boat collisions, and entanglement in fishing nets. Protecting seagrass meadows and reducing human impacts are crucial for their survival.

Fun Fact: Dugongs can stay underwater for up to six minutes before surfacing for air—a skillful breath-holder!

Quick Fact Sheet

  • Common Name(s): Dugong
  • Scientific Name: Dugong dugon
  • Animal Type: Mammal
  • Size: 2.4-3 meters (7.9-9.8 ft)
  • Weight: 250-500 kg (551-1,102 lbs)
  • Lifespan: 70 years or more in the wild
  • Conservation Status: Vulnerable
